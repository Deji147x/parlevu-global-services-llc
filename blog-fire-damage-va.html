What's Covered by Insurance?

When dealing with fire damage, insurance usually covers the repair costs. However, many policies have exclusions or limitations for intentional acts like arson, accidental fires caused by faulty electrical wiring, or damages from the fire department while putting out the blaze.

What's Not Covered?

Some insurers may exclude coverage for damage due to 'accidental' means. This might include damage caused by a malfunctioning smoke detector, clogged air vents, or open flames left unattended during the restoration process.

A Homeowner's Dilemma

Virginia is considered a 'buyer beware' state because of its Residential Property Disclosure Statement. Despite this, sellers can't actively hide known defects. The consequences for selling a damaged home without proper disclosure include severe financial penalties and potential lawsuits.

The Time-Sensitive Nature of Foreclosure

Fast non-judicial foreclosure is prevalent in Virginia. This means that homes can go from notice to auction within under 60 days, emphasizing the importance of swift action for homeowners facing foreclosure or damaged properties.

What Virginia Homeowners Should Know First

Virginia is a 'buyer beware' state using the Residential Property Disclosure Statement, but sellers still cannot actively conceal known defects. That matters no matter how you sell — to a cash buyer, through an agent, or on your own. Being upfront about the property's condition protects you legally and actually speeds up a cash sale, because investors price the repairs in from day one.

Virginia uses fast non-judicial foreclosure — a home can go from notice to auction in under 60 days, so acting early matters more here than almost anywhere. Timing is the single biggest lever you control. The earlier you explore your options, the more of them you have.
