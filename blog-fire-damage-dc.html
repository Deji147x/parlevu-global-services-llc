DC's Seller's Property Condition Disclosure: What You Need to Know

In most residential sales, sellers in Washington DC are required to disclose the property condition to potential buyers. This means you'll need to be upfront about any known defects, including fire damage. Failure to do so can lead to costly lawsuits and damaged credibility.

The Lead-Paint Disclosure: A Local Requirement

As a homeowner in Washington DC, if your home was built before 1978, you're required by law to disclose the presence of lead paint. This is not only a safety concern but also a regulatory one, as failure to comply can result in fines and penalties.

The Challenges of Selling a Fire-Damaged House

Selling a fire-damaged house in Washington DC can be particularly challenging due to the slower judicial foreclosure process compared to Maryland or Virginia. This means you'll need to navigate a more complex system, which can add stress and interest to your situation.

What Washington DC Homeowners Should Know First

DC requires a Seller's Property Condition Disclosure for most residential sales, and pre-1978 homes carry federal lead-paint disclosure duties. That matters no matter how you sell — to a cash buyer, through an agent, or on your own. Being upfront about the property's condition protects you legally and actually speeds up a cash sale, because investors price the repairs in from day one.

DC foreclosures are judicial and slower than in Maryland or Virginia, but interest, fees, and stress compound the longer you wait. Timing is the single biggest lever you control. The earlier you explore your options, the more of them you have.
